818 Files: Hello Girls in a Flood

Written By    Jeremy Oberstein

Just before 1:30 a.m. on March 12, 1928, Louise Gipe got the alert of what would end up being the deadliest man-made disaster in California history.

The newly built St. Francis Dam failed. Billions of gallons of water were already pouring out of San Francisquito Canyon and racing toward the coast, tearing through what is now Santa Clarita and Castaic, killing more than 400 people as it followed the Santa Clara River toward the sea.

Gipe was the overnight telephone operator in nearby Santa Paula. In an instant, her shift changed from passing time amid the quiet hum of midnight to something closer to what we would today consider emergency dispatch. She had no formal training for this but she knew exactly what had to be done.

Years earlier, the arid and rapidly growing city of Los Angeles looked for ways to engineer around its most basic problem. William Mulholland, the Chief Engineer of what was then called the Bureau of Water Works and Supply, had already secured the Owens Valley as a distant source of water, but that solution came with conflict, cost and violence. City leaders wanted something closer, calmer and easier to control.

Mulholland identified San Francisquito Canyon, about 45 miles north of downtown Los Angeles, as an ideal site. The uninhabited canyon near Castaic Lake narrowed downstream and widened upstream, a natural configuration that allowed for a large reservoir behind a comparatively modest dam. On paper, it looked efficient.

But Mulholland had doubts. In 1911, he warned city officials about the “exceedingly rough” geological conditions in the canyon. The project moved forward anyway, with Mulholland leading on-the-ground efforts.

Thousands of workers spent 16 months constructing the 180-foot-tall, 600-foot-wide dam that held 13 billion gallons of Owens Valley water for the city of Los Angeles. The reservoir was gradually filled and reached capacity on March 7, 1928. Almost immediately, engineers noticed small leaks and muddy seepage, signs that water was distressingly moving through the foundation.

On the night of March 12, Ace Hopewell, a carpenter, rode his motorcycle past the dam just before midnight when he heard a rumbling sound, like rocks rolling down a hillside. He stopped, lit a cigarette and checked the hillside, where landslides were not uncommon. The sound faded. Hopewell rode on, the last person to see the dam intact.

Minutes later, fissures became fractures. The dam collapsed completely and the canyon filled with moving water, swallowing everything in its path.

The first formal warning went out at 1:20 a.m. By then, the flood had already traveled roughly 18 miles and crested more than 40 feet, wiping out workers’ camps, killing hundreds.

Faster Than the Flood

When the alert reached her, Louise Gipe didn’t hesitate.

She relayed the warning immediately to police officers Thornton Edwards and Stanley Baker, to city officials and then to homes in the low-lying parts of Santa Paula. The phones became lifelines. For a brief and critical stretch of time, information moved faster than water.

Edwards and Baker mounted their motorcycles and rode through the night, pounding on doors and shouting warnings to families who had gone to bed unaware of the impending catastrophe. Their ride ended at 3:05 a.m., when a 30-foot wall of water tore through Santa Paula on its way toward Ventura and the ocean.

While the motorcycles ran, Louise Gipe stayed at the switchboard.

So did Reicel Jones and Althea Marks, telephone operators downriver in nearby Saticoy. None of them knew how high the water would be by the time it reached their towns. They only knew it had been more than 100 feet high when it left the dam and that their neighbors had to be warned while the lines still worked.

They kept calling.

“Courageous telephone operators… stayed at their perilous posts, calling frantically ahead, trying to outrace the flood by telephone,” historian Cecilia Rasmussen later wrote.

Louise became the most storied of them because Santa Paula was the largest town in the flood’s path and her quick hands and faster thinking saved lives. For nearly six hours, water poured from the top of the dam west toward the Pacific Ocean.

By morning, entire families along the river corridor were gone. Half of the students from Saugus Elementary School were never heard from again. Some victims were found miles inland or decades later. Others were never recovered. But countless others were saved thanks to the operators who became known as the “Hello Girls.”

“Only one thing traveled faster than the floodwaters,” said historian John Nichols, “and that was (their) emergency calls.”

The Cost and the Reckoning

A reckoning followed. Mulholland testified that he had taken “all the care that prudence suggested,” but a jury concluded the failure stemmed from “an error in engineering judgment in determining the foundation,” placing responsibility squarely on the city’s chief engineer. Mulholland stepped down, retreated from public life and died in 1935.

California rewrote its dam safety laws. Engineering oversight was professionalized after officials said such construction “should never be left to the sole judgment of one man, no matter how eminent … for no one is free from error.” Years later, officials found that the dam’s eastern edge sat on an ancient landslide that shifted and plowed into the structure “like a bulldozer blade.”

At that point, Louise Gipe’s job was done. For a few, brief hours, the unlikeliest of heroes answered the call and reacted with the kind of grace under fire that comes when we least expect it.

“Many people are alive today because of heroic acts that were performed on the night of March 12, 1928,” historian Nichols said during a 75th anniversary commemoration.

“Many here survived and went on to have children, grandchildren and great grandchildren. Entire family lines could have been snuffed out without the acts of heroism we honor today.”
