The New Toluca Lake Farmers Market

Written By    James T. Bartlett

Walking or driving alongside Riverside on a Sunday, you might have noticed that the Toluca Lake Farmers Market is back in a new location, with new management, new vendors, a desire to meet the community – and puppies and cookies!

Formerly located behind the Wells Fargo, it’s now down Talofa Avenue alongside the Red Maple, and as well as the expected fresh fruit, vegetables and flowers, there is a rotating variety of vendors offering everything from coffee, cookies and crystals to hummus, juice, paintings, pizzas and tamales.

Wandering around with a smile this recent Sunday was Amber Adame, the mastermind behind this new farmers market, and she sat down with The Tolucan Times to talk about Gaia Society, her non-profit that runs and organizes the market, and why she decided to come back to Toluca Lake.

“I used to vend here years ago – crystals, incense, jewelry – at the other market, and when that closed, I tried other markets as far as Santa Barbara all the way down to like San Diego, but it just wasn’t the same. Some you can kind of just tell it’s like for a money grab, and they don’t really care.”

More than that, she says that she really missed being within the community.

“Toluca Lake is very loving and welcoming, and so I was like: You know what? They’ve been waiting for their farmers market to come back, and I had been thinking about putting one on, and so this is the perfect place.”

Amber, 28, continues to run her Esoteric Beings stall here with help from her family, and feels that because she was a vendor and had been to lots of markets, she knew what was needed – though she admits the process was stressful.

“It’s a lot,” she laughs, “But I think the most important thing is that I like staying organized and staying on top of things. The Market is first-come-first-served, no contracts, which means it will be different every week, and there will always be something new. Like finding an Easter egg.”

“Already the word-of-mouth has been good,” she adds. “More vendors want to come.”

Amber graduated from Mount St. Mary’s University and was set to work in real estate and the mortgage industry, but that “kind of disappeared in the midst of COVID and everything, and I realized that I didn’t want to spend my whole life doing that. So, I kind of went back to like: okay, well what makes me happy? I missed the other vendors – we kind of became a family.”

One of the vendors she takes us to is David Brown – better known as Dapper Dave – who sells desserts and baked goods at the market. He invites people to play him at scissors, rock, paper, with your choice of cookie the prize if you win.

Brown, 30, lives on the border of Toluca Lake and Studio City, and explains that his nickname came about when he performed in Pittsburgh.

“I dressed nice, and the name kinda stuck,” he says, “But this all started after I moved from Pittsburgh to LA for music, and the pandemic hit. I always loved cooking so I started making food for some of my music industry connections. It did well, and so I took it more seriously, worked in some kitchens, went to school and started a business. Eventually one of my clients recommended I started going to farmers markets, and here I am.”

He has been baking professionally for six years now, and notes that in 2022 he went to the Amaury Guichon Pastry Academy in Nevada. His treats use organic ingredients and come in vegan/gluten free options.

And while his favorite cookie is the vanilla bean sugar, the most popular is chocolate chunk followed by “kitchen sink,” which has peanut butter cups, pretzels, and salted caramel.

Another young entrepreneur at what she calls this “hidden gem” farmers market is Chelsie Lynn from Woodland Hills.

Her company Unbare began as “almost a hobby,” she says, but after three months of heavy research and development, she began to sell at local events and farmers markets, initially selling handcrafted soaps and candles.

With more than an eye on the advantages of TikTok, she began formulating products from scratch, “and I rely heavily on real time feedback from customers to fine tune my formulations, making soaps, candles, body butters, lotions, and balms.”

This led to her once-a-month “drops” of new, limited-edition products which, she admits happily, “always sell out fast,” and are, of course, sustainable, cruelty-free, ethically sourced, and free from any harsh ingredients.

As for Amber, she was almost starting from scratch when she began looking into setting up the non-profit.

“So, I actually went to the library,” she says. “I lived closer to Northridge, so I was in their library, and I started reading and reading. By the time I had finished, this felt more like a passion project to me.”

Farmers markets are meant to be non-profit or run by government agencies, and Amber says that once she understood how Burbank’s bureaucracy works, the city officials were extremely helpful.

She initially reached out to a couple of vendors she already knew, though the actual fruit and vegetable vendors were tougher to get, as many travel far longer distances and often visit several markets on different weekends.

Now walking around the market, Amber explains that she and her handful of part-time employees arrive at the site around 6:30am and do a trash clean-up, then make sure that vendors know exactly where they’re located, and help them load in – most are ready to go.

“Talofa Ave is a small space, so we tell the vendors on one side of the street to come in via the alleyway, and the others to turn in from Riverside – so there are no jams.”

They also make sure vendors don’t park in the areas belonging to the adjacent businesses, set up a dining table, and will check in with vendors throughout the day – even handing out bottles of water.

“But we’re here rain or shine,” says Amber. They then do a second trash clean-up before leaving.

The response from customers and vendors alike has been very positive she says, and that she plans to invite musicians and bands too, though she assures us that the music will not be blasting everywhere.

Activities for kids are on her radar as well, and Pawsitive Beginnings puppy adoptions will be back soon too.

“It was extremely popular, and so far, we’ve managed to get like some 60 puppies adopted into the community. We’d love an ice cream vendor in the summer too,” she laughs.

Toluca Farmers Market is open every Sunday from 9am
