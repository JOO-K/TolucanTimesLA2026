The Magic Box

Written By    Johnny Campanella

Bartending in this neighborhood for as long as I have affords me the privilege of meeting all sorts of interesting people, like Brian Nowac. Brian is the founder of Magicbox, a unique company that provides a revolutionary service to the movie, television, social media industries, and more.

Brian recently invited me to check out his amazing invention, Magicbox. I was intrigued and Brian is a great guy, so I drove down Hollywood Way on a warm, sunny January Saturday to take a gander.

Across the street from the Bob Hope Airport, in the Kino Flo parking lot, I exited my car and saw two large black trailers stationed there with the “Magicbox” logo painted in the ROY G. BIV color spectrum on their sides. One trailer was unfolded and the other one’s walls were packed up.

A mutual friend and neighborhood fixture, J.P. Kisob, greeted me (if you’re a local resident, you’ve seen J.P. somewhere in Toluca Lake). He escorted me into a luxurious production office trailer on loan from industry transportation giant, Galpin. I used to pick up box trucks at 5am from the Galpin lot in Hollywood many moons ago when I was a lowly P.A.! Instant street cred.

“They loan the trailer when Brian does his demonstrations,” J.P. told me. “Pretty swanky, man,” I replied. J.P. chuckled. We talked for a while as we waited for Brian to finish up with a group of producers inside the unfolded Magicbox.

J.P. works in Sales & Marketing for Magicbox. Prior to that, he was a Production Manager on commercials. He’s a professional and a genuine, supportive friend to Brian. I asked J.P. what exactly Magicbox is. He gave me broad-strokes. J.P. started by explaining one specific Magicbox application: shooting car process driving scenes. “That’s massive. There’s always a driving scene,” I exclaimed. J.P. nodded, “Exactly.” Driving scenes are staples. They’re a great way to deliver exposition, geographically move characters, and advance narrative all at once. I began to wrap my head around the Magicbox concept.

“Put any car inside Magicbox, and the LED screens project whatever street you want. Brian works with companies that have vast libraries of usable backgrounds. From Timbuktu to Morocco to New York City or Sunset Blvd., they provide the necessary plates. Also, they have vehicles rigged with cameras, as well as drones that can shoot custom plates for clients. Or, if a production wants to use their own backgrounds, Magicbox gives exact specifications for them to shoot their own plates.” I sat back on the sofa and envisioned shooting an Indiana Jones–style driving scene on the streets of Timbuktu, but in Burbank, and inside Magicbox.

That really made me think. Being a fan of The Mandalorian, I had a notion of what Brian was up to with Magicbox. I asked J.P., “Is Magicbox technology akin to how The Mandalorian was shot? Like the LED projections they used to render the backgrounds in that show?” In his gracious manner, J.P. said, “It’s better if Brian explains. It’s his company, his vision.”

After a while, Brian emerged from the Magicbox with his attendees. A quick Q&A was followed by information exchanges, handshakes, and smiles. When business concluded, Brian greeted me.

“Brian, what’s going on here?” I puzzled. He replied, “The short version is that we build these semi-trailers that look like that when they’re on the road (he pointed at the folded up Magicbox), and when we get to wherever we’re going, which is anywhere across the country and into Mexico and Canada, we drop the trailer and it takes about 35–45 minutes to transform into this.” Brian gestured toward the unfolded Magicbox trailer.

“You haul these all across North America?” I asked. J.P. interjected, “Brian recently went to the University of Pittsburgh to work with their athletic department, shooting their media content. He is going to Arizona soon to work with the San Francisco Giants at Spring Training.” Brian’s vision was becoming clear. Magicbox is like a Swiss Army Knife—mobile, versatile, and with numerous applications.

Brian led me in for show-and-tell. Inside the Magicbox, a Land Rover was parked on a huge hydraulic turntable, surrounded by state-of-the-art LED screens, with one hanging above the car. The screens displayed synced-up, moving visuals of a familiar local, tree-lined street that could be viewed from every angle. I recognized the street. I’ve driven it a hundred times. I felt my intuition was on point, so I got bold.

“So, this is what they did for The Mandalorian, but instead of suburban Studio City, they projected a weird planet in the background, right?” I asked. “Yeah, it’s called LED Volume, which is something that ILM developed back in 2018 for that show,” he informed. “Magicbox is a mobile version of the same technology that we developed. I saw what they did and I wanted to use it for my productions, but it cost tens of millions of dollars to build one and house it permanently on a soundstage. And, to rent that stage for one day, it was like $250K. I owned an ad agency and a production company in San Francisco at the time, so I decided to build one that I could afford to use.”

I asked, “Can you give me a watered-down, layman’s explanation?” He replied, “I’ll sum it up like this: Magicbox is a mobile LED studio that can go anywhere a production might be—a stage, a location, an event, wherever. It transforms quickly from a trailer into a soundstage with an LED Volume inside. You can get your shots in a controlled environment, move on, and it’s relatively low cost.” That’s pretty cool.

“Actors must like it, especially for the driving scenes, right?” I asked. “They love it. It’s convenient and safe. It also makes things easy for directors and cinematographers. No moving vehicles and instant resets,” Brian explained. I pondered, “So they can focus on performance and nuance without the hassle, and still capture a totally believable visual sequence?” Brian replied, “That’s the intent. We make it feel as real as possible for the actors. For example, we’ll rig a monitor inside the car so they can see where the turns in the road are and manipulate the steering wheel and react appropriately. It feels natural, it’s in a controlled environment, and they can focus on performance.”

I fixated on the Land Rover as the LED screens projected a realistic simulation of the Las Vegas Strip. I snapped out of it, but it felt very real. “Does anyone ever get motion sickness?” I asked. Brian laughed, “Maybe about one in fifty. It’s not a chronic issue at all, but it can happen.” I nodded and told him, “My compliments to the chef. Any physical reaction is a testament to you for delivering what you promise.” Brian gave a humble, yet confident shrug.

Magicbox’s mobility and cost efficiency have massive implications, especially for independent films working with minimal budgets. Filmmakers can utilize cutting-edge technology, not only to up production value, but to cut costs. Add in practical foreground sets and tangible props, and any location or environment is accessible through Magicbox technology. Indie filmmakers are free to go big and not limit their vision due to budget constraints. Think of it like an early Robert Rodriguez film on steroids.

Brian showed me a scene done for a production that takes place on the Catalina Island pier. “It would have been a nightmare and cost a fortune to send an entire crew there. We sent two guys out in the morning to shoot plates, they brought them back that afternoon, we put them up on the walls, and shot the scene. We built a physical portion of wood railing that was matched up with the screen and had a fan blowing,” Brian explained. They executed all of that on the same day.

Shooting with two cameras, at six different angles, and doing three full reads of a 5-page script at each angle, they set up, shot, and struck in 2 hours and 45 minutes, capturing 190 minutes of coverage. I watched the scene, and I gotta say, the raw composite footage looked fantastic.

“Why hasn’t this caught on like wildfire?” I asked. “Hopefully it will,” Brian replied. Well, he’s certainly pounding the pavement hosting demonstrations each week. “We’re doing a lot of TV right now—High Potential, 9-1-1, Grey’s Anatomy, Matlock... and commercials, sports, and other things. We want to work more with independent films too. I provide this technology to empower smaller productions and also service the big ones.”

Another unique Magicbox detail is that Brian vertically integrated user-friendly software which can be manipulated and reconfigured to meet the needs of each department. And, depending on the complexity, turnaround can be as quick as a request made before lunch and the adjustment ready by the time the A.D. yells, “We’re back!”

With sincere consideration to the state of things, Brian said, “We’re not here to take jobs. We want to enable everyone on set to work faster and more efficiently. We want to help create more productions.” You have to rally around that.

Brian Nowac’s Magicbox is truly innovative and can revolutionize the way movies and television are produced and shot. Magicbox is a supplement, not a replacement, and it has a multitude of applications. For instance, the folded up Magicbox trailer beside the LED studio trailer has a different design and intended use. When it’s unpacked, it’s a ready-to-go set piece for concerts, seminars, live TV broadcasts. Magicbox provides endless possibilities.

Brian created an amazing tool, and made it accessible to productions big and small. The high-end quality, diverse capability, and mobile convenience of Magicbox allows productions and filmmakers to dream big and let their imaginations run wild. Inspiring, innovative, and definitely empowering.
