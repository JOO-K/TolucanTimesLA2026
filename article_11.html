One of the Boys

Written By    Trevor Nystrom

April 2026

If Hollywood had a Swiss Army knife, it would look a lot like Clint Howard. From the bridge of the Starship Enterprise to the grit of independent horror, Clint’s one-of-a-kind personality has made him a permanent fixture in the entertainment industry. Yet, when the Tolucan Times pulled up a chair next to him at Patys, his sprawling filmography slowly faded into the background. Here, in the heart of Toluca Lake, the unforgettable character actor with over 250 film and television credits under his belt, suddenly transformed into something much simpler—a local!

Born in Burbank in 1959, Clint Howard began his journey in show business as a toddler, debuting on The Andy Griffith Show alongside his brother, Ron. By the age of eight, he was already a household name starring as Mark Wedloe in Gentle Ben, but it was his transition into one of Hollywood’s most resilient character actors that defined his historic, sixty-year career. As a true utility player, Clint’s filmography is a staggering collection of credits that bridge the gap between major blockbusters like Apollo 13 and cult classics like The Ice Cream Man and Rock ’n’ Roll High School. His ability to lean into offbeat, eccentric roles has made him a standout icon in franchises like Star Trek, where he has appeared in five different iterations of the series, all while his roots remained firmly planted in the neighborhood.

Guided by the pragmatic work ethic of his father, Rance, Clint has navigated a lifelong career with a rare authenticity, proving that even in the bustling world of the show business, one can still remain just a kid from the block.

“I’ve been coming to Patys since 1968. There used to be the short order cook right there by the counter. Every Sunday morning, Dad would throw Ron and me some money—so Mom and Dad could have a little time alone. We lived over there on Clybourn, so we’d walk up here and watch the guy make us breakfast. Ron and I were absolutely fascinated by it,” he recalls.

Clint attended David Starr Jordan Junior High (now Dolores Huerta Middle School) in Burbank. Traversing the typical trials of adolescence while already being a seasoned professional in the industry, his time at the Mariposa Street campus proved a foundational aspect of his upbringing, not just as an actor, but as a storyteller. “In junior high, me and my best friend signed up for journalism. Mr. Campbell taught us the craft and laid the foundation for me to become a writer. Learning the who, what, when, where, how, and why—how to write a lead and interview people—it was all so important to me.”

That local immersion continued through his teens at John Burroughs High School, where Clint traded the soundstage for the pitcher’s mound, as a two-year varsity letterman for the Burroughs Indians, he and his brother both became hometown heroes in almost all of their sporting endeavors. And like most high schoolers, Clint also played in a band. “Ron is kind of a refined jazz orchestra and I’m like a punk rocker. Ron was in the Happy Days and I was in the Kempsters,” Clint said with a chuckle, adding, “On Kemp Street in Toluca Woods is where me and my buddies started the band Clint Howard and the Kempsters.”
“Our real last name isn’t Howard,” Clint says with a smirk.

“It was all because of my dad’s dream of being in show business. He just didn’t see himself being Harold Beckenholdt from Oklahoma. Why Howard? No one really knows. My aunt speculated there may have been a character in a movie named Rance, but Dad never really said.”

Rance Howard was the unsung backbone of the Howard acting dynasty, a veteran performer whose career spanned seven decades and over 280 acting credits, from Chinatown to Nebraska. More than just a versatile actor, he was the pragmatic architect of his sons’ success, instilling a blue-collar work ethic that prioritized the craft of storytelling over the allure of fame. Though he was a constant regular on nearly every film set, Rance remained a deeply grounded figure, the moral north star for the entire Howard family.

“When Dad passed away, we knew we needed to do something to honor him. People had tugged at Ron for years to write a memoir, but he wasn’t interested,” Clint explains.
Ron Howard’s career is a rare Hollywood odyssey, evolving from his iconic childhood role as Opie Taylor in The Andy Griffith Show, to Richie Cunningham in Happy Days as a teen, into one of the industry’s most respected directors in the history of cinema. After proving his mettle with hits like Splash and Cocoon, Ron ascended to the A-list with masterpieces like Apollo 13 and the Academy Award-winning A Beautiful Mind.

It was Tom Hanks who told Ron, “If you wrote a book, you should write one about your childhood, because ultimately everybody wants to know what it was like being a kid actor.” And then he said, “You should get your brother involved, because Clint played with a bear when he was a kid.”

In 2021, Clint and Ron released The Boys, a dual memoir that offers a candid reflection on growing up in Hollywood. The narrative functions as a dialogue between brothers, contrasting Ron’s steady ascent toward directing with Clint’s gritty journey as a prolific character actor. The book also serves as a heartfelt tribute to their parents, Rance and Jean, and in my opinion, stands as one of the greatest love letters to Toluca Lake ever written. “Ninety-nine point five percent of everything we say in the book is true. We appreciated our childhood, and it just so happened to take place here.”

The Boys is a must-read for anyone who lives in Toluca Lake because of its wholesome nostalgia and charm. It beautifully captures the neighborhood’s transformation from a quiet studio suburb into the pulsing heartbeat of the entertainment industry. The memoir is also filled with local landmarks like Smoke House and Barone’s and personal anecdotes that illustrate how the streets of the Valley provided a grounded sanctuary for the Howard family amidst the whirlwind of their ballooning Hollywood fame. It is truly a profound tribute to the community that helped shape two of the industry’s most enduring figures, proving that the most important stories often happen right in our own backyard.

“It included our lives living here in Toluca Lake, it included our lives when Ron was coaching basketball and Toluca Lake Little League, going to Burroughs High School. You know, Ron met his high school sweetheart at Burroughs,” Clint asserted.
Clint is currently in pre-production for his newest film, Another Ice Cream Man, a sequel to his 1995 cult classic. Only this time, he’s playing a new, different murderer rather than a direct continuation of his Gregory Tudor character. His brother Ron was the first person to sign on to the project. “For a film to really work, the movie gods have to come down and kiss it. Very talented people can have great material, a perfect cast, and a good crew, and for whatever reason, the timing doesn’t quite work. It’ll be a good movie, but it won’t be a super-successful movie. But it’s really the story,” Clint says.

As the morning rush at Patys hums around him, it becomes clear that Clint’s greatest role isn’t Balok, or an ice cream slinging serial killer, or even just the boy with the bear, it’s the role of the keeper of the flame for what has always made Toluca Lake great.

In a town infamous for its short-term memory, Clint stands as a living bridge between the old studio lots to the modern neighborhood that he still calls home. Whether he’s stepping onto a film set or simply walking around the familiar streets of our beloved Valley, he carries with him the wisdom of Rance and Jean and the shared history of The Boys with his brother Ron. Our breakfast with Clint reminds us that sometimes the best stories aren’t just found in scripts, but in the neighbors who tell them.
