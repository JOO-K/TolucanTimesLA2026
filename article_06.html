The Man, The myth, Your Neighbor: Kato Kaelin

Written By    Gino Renzulli

Recently, I had the pleasure of spending nearly three hours getting to know Kato Kaelin. Some readers, especially those born after 1990, may not be immediately familiar with Kato, while readers over the age of 35 will likely have no trouble recalling America’s most famous guest house dweller. Part of my desire to interview Kato stemmed from getting to know him through his online presence and discovering what a light-hearted, entertaining guy he is. I immediately recognized the unfortunate irony that such a fun-loving person should be so inextricably attached to such a horrific event.

As such, I feel almost guilty even providing the basic context for Kato Kaelin’s fame. Kato Kaelin served as a witness in the O.J. Simpson murder trial in 1995, an event that became one of the largest media spectacles this country has ever seen. At the time of the crime, Kato was living in Simpson’s guest house. Almost overnight, he became a household name, and his memorable name, look, and mythos as the man in the back house became part of American cultural lore.

But that’s not who Kato wanted to be when he moved to Los Angeles in 1984, and that’s not how I’d like Tolucan readers to know him. Kato is a jovial, energetic, almost impish man who, in a more just world, would be more famous than he is now and for better reasons. Let’s get to know Kato Kaelin, who has been a Toluca Lake neighbor for over 20 years, for who he is and not for something that just so happened to involve him more than 30 years ago.

Kato is a multifaceted entertainer. An actor first, he has also spent decades hosting radio shows dating back to his college days in Wisconsin. For years, he emceed for Wizard World, the largest traveling comic convention, working all around the country in the process. Today, he hosts a true crime podcast called One Degree of Scandalous with his co-host Tom Zenner.

“We’ve been at it for three years, and I say the same thing all the time. After three years, we’ve hit the light at the end of the tunnel: advertisers. Finally, after persevering and working our asses off.”

Kato and Tom do their show from West Coast Creative Studio in Hollywood.
“It’s been such a challenge because we write the show, we get great guests, and now we’re getting a kind of a buzz, so it’s been great, but it’s taken some time.”

The podcast often features interviews with guests who, like Kato, have some connection to major crimes or scandals.

“One of our basic things we do, which is kind of cool, and our viewers love it, is we go to crime scenes where we take a tour, and then we have the drones. We did the Hillside Stranger with Detective Tom Lange and Bob Souza.”

Kato emanates enthusiasm as he tells me about his podcast. He has come to make the most of the hand he’s been dealt, though it took a long time for him to embrace his undeniable connection to true crime.
“Everything we do [on the podcast] has just been really cathartic for me because of the fact that it’s crime. I never wanted to embrace it. I hated being attached to the Simpson trial because I was this person of light and fun and happiness. And I was always reading for films. That was my life, to be an actor. And next thing you know, I’m thrust into the biggest trial of the century.”

“By no fault of my own, I became involved in this thing with the double edged sword of negativity and fame.”
“My wish of coming from Milwaukee, Wisconsin was: ‘Who doesn’t want to be famous? You kidding? Be on TV?’ But everything happened to me backwards. I became famous before people could say, ‘he has talent.’ Although, I had commercials, and I had my SAG card already.”

Growing up, Kato’s father owned two restaurants in Milwaukee along with a liquor distributorship. Although, as Kato was quick to point out, “My dad didn’t drink.” Both of his parents served in World War II, his father as a soldier and his mother as a nurse. He described his family as tight-knit and said he spoke with them often when he first moved to California.

“It was the loneliest time in my life because I didn’t drink or do drugs. Other people would get together to drink, so I would end up alone, and I hated it.”

At that time, Kato was taking acting classes under the late Scott Arthur Allen. It wasn’t all bad. Kato booked a national Coca-Cola commercial from his very first audition, an incredibly rare feat.
Kato had moved out to Los Angeles from his home state of Wisconsin around 1980 to pursue acting, but he was also an avid baseball player. He played baseball at his first college, the University of Wisconsin–Eau Claire. He tried out at Cal State Fullerton but admits the other guys were simply too big for him.
“I didn’t get whiskers until I was 38,” Kato said before repeating the phrase by yelling it into my phone that was recording our conversation to remove any doubt that it stood on the record.
Although baseball didn’t pan out professionally, he still loves watching and is an avid Wisconsin sports fan across the board.

As we chatted by the bar at the Hotel Amarano, where we met, Kato frequently waved at friends he knew and even engaged strangers in playful conversations, trying both to connect with them and make them laugh. That’s who Kato is. He’s a man of the people. When I told him that my mom actually worked on Ryan Murphy’s television dramatization of the Simpson trial, he insisted we call her, and they proceeded to talk for a while about some of the people he knew who worked on the show.

All is well for Kato, and he deserves it. He is starring in a new horror film titled A Soldier’s Descent, which is currently in post-production. He is happily married to his wife, Sofie Xue, who works at Compass Toluca Lake under Craig Strong’s team right here in Toluca Lake, and his podcast is getting more buzz than ever.
If you see Kato around the neighborhood, be sure to say hello. You won’t regret it!
