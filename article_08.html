Backlot Business

Written By    Trevor Nystrom

For nearly a century, Radford Studios has been more than just a collection of soundstages, it’s been the pulse of Hollywood. From the slapstick era of Mack Sennett to the “must-see” heights of Seinfeld, the local legendary lot on Radford Avenue wasn’t just a workplace, it was the identity of Studio City. When news broke that Hackman Capital Partners was ceding their keys to Goldman Sachs after a staggering $1.1 billion default, that once-healthy heartbeat began felt dangerously faint.

Mack Sennett Studios, Republic Pictures, CBS Radford— nicknamed “Hit City” after its success with Gunsmoke, Gilligan’s Island, and The Mary Tyler Moore Show —whatever you want to call it, the infamous backlot has been and will always be a cornerstone of Studio City. With recent programs like Parks and Recreation, Brooklyn Nine Nine, and The Conners occupying its sacred space so integral to the community that the suburb was officially named in its honor, it would be a deadly blow to our local economy. Originally founded in 1928 by Sennett, the “King of Comedy,” when he moved from Echo Park to Studio City, transforming a humble lettuce farm into a production titan. The lot eventually weathered bankruptcy to become Republic Pictures, the B-movie factory that launched the careers of John Wayne and Roy Rogers. Today, the 55-acre lot remains a ghost town of what it once was, a mere glimmer of Hollywood history forgotten by the times.

This collapse should serve as a “check-engine light” for the Los Angeles economy. Two years ago, we were promised a $1 billion modernization of a “New Hit City.” However, those renderings are nothing more than mere fantasies of disillusionment now. Ownership blames high interest rates and production droughts, but on Ventura Boulevard, the crisis is personal. A 63% occupancy rate isn’t just a statistic, it’s empty tables at restaurants and silence in nearby coffee shops. When revenue covers only 21% of debt, the community doesn’t just lose a developer, it loses an anchor.

There is a bitter irony in the fact that the very thing many neighbors feared, the massive, traffic-inducing expansion and parking problems, is now dead on arrival. Yet “victory” for the anti-development crowd feels hollow when the alternative is a bank-owned property in limbo. Goldman Sachs is in the business of assets, not artistry. While Hackman Capital was a developer with a specific vision for the future of Studio City, even if it seemed controversial to some, that prosperous vision is now gone. We’ve already seen reports of the lot becoming “decrepit” and “dangerous,” on February 6th, 2024, Marvel’s Wonder Man crew member, Juan Carlos “Spike” Osorio, died after falling from a catwalk, leading to a $36,000 fine against Disney for the deteriorated catwalk, with his family and union, IATSE, mourning the needless loss and advocation for greater set safety.

The Radford default should serve as a warning that Los Angeles can no longer take the entertainment industry for granted. When production flees, the dry cleaners, florists, and homeowners suffer first. To keep our industry alive, we need more than a landlord with deep pockets, we need a reckoning on how to keep Hollywood at home. If we fail, the only thing filmed on Radford Avenue will be a documentary about its glory days after it’s been torn down. The tragedy isn’t that the expansion failed, it’s the silence that has taken its place in Studio City.
